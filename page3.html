The Hidden Perpetrators

Exposing the True Perpetrators: Dean Crawford and Alex Cancinos

To understand how Elmo's DNA ended up on a piece of evidence, you have to look at the people who actually committed the crimes and the physical connection they shared with him. These cases are not isolated; they are connected by undeniable physical evidence.

The Physical Connection Between the Attacks

The New Jersey victim (N.W.) was attacked by two men; her phone was taken. Months later, her SIM card was discovered in another crime in New York—the H.T. crime.

In the H.T. crime, a cell phone was left at the scene by the attacker, described as a 6-ft white male with blue eyes. N.W.’s stolen SIM card was enclosed in that cell phone. The DNA derived from that cell phone matched the stocking found in the New Jersey A.T. crime. This creates the scientific "bridge" that proves the same perpetrators were involved in both scenes.

4 The Physical Connection: The Workplace

Elmo Rivadeneira rented a garage in Kearny where he worked on cars. His coworker was a man named Alex Cancinos. Cancinos was the accomplice of Dean Crawford—the man who left Elmo's disconnected cell phone and DNA at the H.T. crime scene.

Because Cancinos worked alongside Elmo, he had unrestricted access to Elmo’s vehicles and personal belongings. This is the physical link the jury never got to see because the prosecution withheld the fact that Dean Crawford had been identified by both H.T. and N.W., and that Elmo did not match the H.T. description.

Alex Cancinos was allowed to testify against Elmo about a "letter" he supposedly burned—a letter he claimed was mailed to him asking him to plant DNA. By doing so, Cancinos secured the dismissal of his own first-degree kidnapping and rape charges. He traded Elmo’s freedom for his own, spinning a fiction story about a "burned letter" that only he had seen.

Hopefully, the Federal District Court gives Elmo the relief he deserves because, under Brady v. Maryland, 373 U.S. 83 (1963), the government has a constitutional duty to turn over evidence that favors the defendant. Failing to disclose a report that identifies a different man’s DNA is the ultimate violation of due process.
